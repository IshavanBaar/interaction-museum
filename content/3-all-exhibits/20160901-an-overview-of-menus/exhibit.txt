Writings: <h2><i>We have become very familiar with menus, and often simply take them from existing frameworks. Yet, there are so many examples of badly designed menus. What menus exist, and how do we choose between them?</i></h2><p>Menus are a way for a user to select between a set of operations. The set can have a fixed or a variable size, and can be hierarchical. Menus have been around for a long time. The NLS Augment system, showcased by Douglas Engelbart in the famous “mother of all demos”, already used menus for <a href="../link-jumping">Link Jumping</a>.</p><div class="medium-insert-images medium-insert-images-wide"><figure contenteditable="false"><img src="http://localhost/interaction-museum/content/2-all-techniques/20160901-link-jumping/ezgif.com-crop-2.gif" class=""><p class="thumbnail-caption">Link Jumping</p><figcaption contenteditable="true" class="">In this example, the user can click on the items of a list and jump to a sublist page.</figcaption></figure></div><p><span style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px;">Menus are part of the current standard that we call </span><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WIMP_(computing)" style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px; background-color: rgb(255, 255, 255);">WIMP interfaces</a><span style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px;">: </span><b style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px;">W</b><span style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px;">indows, </span><b style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px;">I</b><span style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px;">cons, </span><b style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px;">M</b><span style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px;">enus and </span><b style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px;">P</b><span style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px;">ointing. While these were originally metaphors for physical objects, we now use their digital equivalents in our everyday language. WIMP interfaces are part of direct manipulation, of which </span><a href="../drag-and-drop" style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px; background-color: rgb(255, 255, 255);">Drag-and-Drop</a><span style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px;"> is the most known example.</span><br></p><div class="medium-insert-images medium-insert-images-wide"><figure contenteditable="false"><img src="http://localhost/interaction-museum/content/2-all-techniques/20160901-drag-and-drop/drag-and-drop.gif" class=""><p class="thumbnail-caption">Drag-and-Drop</p><figcaption contenteditable="true" class="">Drag and drop color blobs to give the headset a new color.</figcaption></figure></div><p>So what different menus are there? The classical example is a hierarchical <a href="../drop-down-menu">Drop-Down Menu</a>. But the more items, the worse this menu performs:</p><blockquote><i>“Mega menus are the Where’s Waldo of navigation. You’re making the user do all the work."</i>  (<a href="http://www.blindfiveyearold.com/mega-menus-are-mega-awful">AJ Kohn</a>)&nbsp;</blockquote><p class=""><div class="medium-insert-images medium-insert-images-grid"><figure contenteditable="false"><img src="http://localhost/interaction-museum/content/2-all-techniques/20160901-drop-down-menu/ezgif.com-video-to-gif.gif" class=""><p class="thumbnail-caption">Drop-Down Menu</p></figure></div></p><p class=""><br></p><p>This is why researchers have proposed several improvements:</p><p class=""><br></p><div class="medium-insert-images medium-insert-images-right"><figure contenteditable="false"><img src="http://localhost/interaction-museum/content/2-all-techniques/20160901-ephemeral-menu/ezgif.com-crop-1.gif" class=""><p class="thumbnail-caption">Ephemeral Menu</p><figcaption contenteditable="true" class="">Notice that some items appear first</figcaption></figure></div><div class="medium-insert-images medium-insert-images-left"><figure contenteditable="false"><img src="http://localhost/interaction-museum/content/2-all-techniques/20160901-bubbling-menu/ezgif.com-crop.gif" class=""><p class="thumbnail-caption">Bubbling Menu</p><figcaption contenteditable="true" class="">Guiding of the cursor to some menu items</figcaption></figure></div><p>A <a href="../bubbling-menu">Bubbling Menu</a> guides the cursor faster to frequently used items, using <a href="../bubble-cursor">Bubble Cursor </a>selection. An&nbsp;<a href="../ephemeral-menu">Ephemeral Menu</a>&nbsp;predicts what items will be chosen, and displays these first.</p><p><a href="../pie-menu">Pie Menus</a> (or <i>Radial Menus</i>) are a faster alternative. Menu items are placed in a circle around the cursor, or around a fixed position. In the first case, the items are closer to the cursor and have a bigger target size than in traditional <i>Drop-Down Menus</i>, resulting in shorter seeking time and lower error rates. But even Pie Menus are limited to around 7 items since they take too much space.<span style="color: rgb(204, 204, 204); font-size: 0.8em; font-style: italic; text-align: center; letter-spacing: 0.1px; line-height: 1.846;">&nbsp;</span></p><div class="medium-insert-images medium-insert-images-wide"><figure contenteditable="false"><img src="http://localhost/interaction-museum/content/2-all-techniques/20160901-pie-menu/pie_menu.gif" class=""><p class="thumbnail-caption">Pie Menu</p></figure></div><p><span style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px;">This is where <a href="../marking-menu">Marking Menus</a> come in. Marking Menus are<i> Pie Menus</i> where you use straight stroke gestures to select the items. In this way, they allow for a very quick selection, which can also have multiple levels of a hierarchy. They are very suitable for expert users, such as industrial or graphical designers. For example, they are used in design programs such as Autodesk's&nbsp;<a href="http://www.autodesk.com/products/maya/overview">Maya</a> or <a href="http://www.autodesk.nl/products/3ds-max/overview">3ds Max</a>&nbsp;</span><span style="letter-spacing: 0.1px; line-height: 1.5;">to quickly select important commands in combination with keyboard and mouse buttons.&nbsp;</span></p><p class=""><div class="medium-insert-images medium-insert-images-wide"><figure contenteditable="false"><img src="http://localhost/interaction-museum/content/2-all-techniques/20160901-marking-menu/marking_menu.gif" class=""><p class="thumbnail-caption">Marking Menu</p><figcaption contenteditable="true" class="">Try this menu out on&nbsp;http://ishavanbaar.com/dojo-markingmenu/demo2.html</figcaption></figure></div></p><p><span style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px;">There are many more examples of menus, such menus that are optimized for the use on mobile phones (Swiss Army Menu). Also, Gilles Bailly owns </span><a href="http://www.gillesbailly.fr/menua/" style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px; background-color: rgb(255, 255, 255);">a very useful collection of menus</a><span style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px;">&nbsp;that are developed by research.</span><br></p><p class="medium-insert-active"><br></p><div class="medium-insert-images medium-insert-images-wide"><figure contenteditable="false"><img src="http://localhost/interaction-museum/content/2-all-techniques/20160901-swiss-army-menu/swiss-army.gif" class=""><p class="thumbnail-caption">Swiss Army Menu</p><figcaption contenteditable="true" class="">This menu is optimized for small screens.</figcaption></figure></div><p class=""><span style="line-height: 1.5; letter-spacing: 0.1px;">So, next time: take some time and THINK when you choose your menu! There exist some really good and interesting alternatives :)</span><br></p><p><i>Do you have any suggestions? Please send them over to me <a href="mailto:mbl@lri.fr">here</a>.&nbsp;</i></p>
